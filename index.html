About

I’m a computer science and economics double-major at Dartmouth College. Though I only started coding a year ago, computer science has quickly become my favorite subject and pastime. Recently I’ve enjoyed exploring web development (this website is a product of that exploration) and building basic video games. I've also become a teacher’s assistant for two computer science professors. Outside the realm of cs, I enjoy playing on the ultimate team, intramural sports, hiking (I recently hiked the Tour de Mont Blanc), basketball, and musicals.

    Little Rascals, April 2014

    Controlling a classroom full of small, hyperactive miscreants is a task unto itself. Teaching them anything of substance is another matter entirely. I never grasped the difficulty of teaching until I tried it myself at Westshire Elementary School in West Fairlee, Vermont. I taught three separate hour-long classes every week for nine weeks, plus an hour of prep time. At Westshire, I learned why teaching is such a challenging endeavor. Good teachers balance discipline with fun; I could not. I was unable to have fun with my students without letting them get out of control. I eventually resorted to bribing them. When we played a learning game, I rewarded the winner with candy. This strategy worked well for me, and I eventually gave everyone in my class a reward for trying, not just the “winners.” My students now know over 100 Spanish words and phrases.

    I learned that while I may be a mediocre teacher, my teachers are amazing. Because of my experience teaching Spanish, I have a deeper appreciation for all of them.

    Final Reflection From The Mountain School, 29 May 2014

    The Mountain School is a family of teachers, students, firemen, athletes, musicians, and academics, hidden in rural Vershire, Vermont. Another school like it can never exist. The people shape the place as much as the place shapes the people. We come from different backgrounds yet we’re remarkably the same. It takes a certain type of person to want to live here. We are people aiming to improve ourselves and those around us. We are people with a purpose.

    Robert Frost wrote, “My object in living is to unite my avocation and my vocation.” Because of the individuals here, I know what that looks like. It looks like Sugar-man Sam throwing wood into the evaporator, Alden preforming The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock in English class, and Kathy unveiling her cinnamon rolls at snack time. I’m eager to learn here because I glean passion from the people surrounding me. My English teacher loves poetry so I’ve learned to like it. Never before have I memorized a poem out of my own free will. Alden’s class is the first English class since the third grade that I’ve enjoyed.

    I think part of it has to do with seeing teachers from more than one angle. We live with them and we meet their families. We become each others’ families. They are our advisors, friends, and mentors. Over dinner, I can discuss my French assignment with Kareen. When I have a problem, I can knock on her door and she’s happy to help. There’s no better way to end the day than in her company at dorm-meeting. Teachers are there to comfort us yet they also challenge us. I’ve been pushed further academically than ever before. And I’ve also been pushed to feel mutually responsible for everything that happens here.

    One thursday night I saw Jack in the kitchen. He was cleaning up after the dish crew that had left the place in disarray. My dish crew. He was sacrificing his evening to clean up after some teenagers who were in too much a hurry to do their part. He didn’t expect an apology and he wasn’t angry. I felt morally inclined to help him and in that moment, I learned something no other school can teach.

    Jack does so many jobs that mostly go unnoticed. He’s teaching a silent lesson that goes beyond the Mountain School. He makes me wonder how much other people do for me that I don’t know about. It’s a comforting thought and I know the only way I can repay that favor is by doing my own part, and more.

    When I hear myself say “why haven’t they cleaned this up yet,” I have to stop myself. The truth is “why haven’t I cleaned it up yet?” When the kitchen is left untidy, everyone should feel complicity because it’s all of our responsibilities to keep this place clean.

    This is why the Mountain School feels like home. Teachers and students alike work together to achieve a common goal. Every little thing we do is for each other, whether it’s washing counters, splitting wood, or scrubbing potatoes. At the Mountain School, we cultivate land and good character. It’s our place and we take care of it.

    Independence Day, 6 June 2014

    It was an act of rebellion, driving to Honey Locust Farm. I was about to start my first real job. I fidgeted, pondering the consequences of turning back. What propelled me forward was fear - of facing my dad, admitting failure.

    I had ignored my father's advice by accepting this job. The day we drove to Sunny Meadow for my interview, he let me know he wasn't impressed with the farmer, Jake. Afterwards my dad drove me to another, more-established farm. I refused to get out of the car. The harder he pushed, the more I resisted.

    You could say it was stubbornness that got me to work on Day One. I call it determination. I turned into the driveway, parked, wiped the sweat off my hands. Jake was leaning against a Chevy pickup loaded with crates of silent, cowering roosters. I walked over. We shook hands, exchanging awkward pleasantries.

    Jake instructed me to pull a chicken from its crate. It jerked and squawked. It tried to open its wings; I clamped them shut. Jake led me to the 'gallows,' a steel cone with the bottom cut off. He stuffed my chicken into the gallows upside down; its head protruded from the hole in the bottom, while its feet stuck out the top. Deftly, Jake pulled a knife and slit the chicken's throat. My stomach tensed. The steel cone gyrated as the chicken convulsed. Its neck whipped around, spewing blood. Dark splotches spread across my T-shirt and shorts. Then the chicken hung limp.

    Jake seized another chicken, stuffing it into the gallows. He gave me the knife and gestured expectantly. Could I perform the heinous act? I was the kid who used to cry whenever my cat killed a bird. And there I was, about to commit murder. What would Jake do if I refused? I almost quit on the spot; instead, I willed my hand to work.

    Words can't describe what happened next; the outcome was unspeakably horrible.

    Jake smirked. "You messed the poor guy up," he scolded. He pulled the knife from my clammy hands and decapitated the chicken. I felt my cheeks turn crimson.

    I stared at the concrete, strewn with blood and feathers. I was twenty minutes into a ten-hour day, and I had broken a moral barrier. It was a triumph of sorts, but not one that I wished to repeat.

    Jake didn't ask me to kill another chicken. Instead, he handed me over to Mary, a portly woman who carried her sleeping baby strapped to her back. Mary taught me to eviscerate. We worked together like a well-oiled machine; she killed and de-feathered the chickens while I gutted them. Soon I had evisceration down to a science. But to say the day flew by would be a lie. When your hair is matted with blood, and your hands reek with the stench of chicken guts, time passes slowly.

    After work I went out with friends. They all ordered chicken wings; I opted for nachos. Weeks would pass before I had an appetite for chicken again. But the next morning, I was back at the farm, ready for work.

    Jake greeted me with a smile instead of a smirk. I had passed the test. Day Two at the farm marked the beginning of a longstanding friendship.

    Jake and his wife Leah treated me like family. They invited me often for dinner, I gave Leah mandolin lessons, and Jake offered me bushels of free produce. Yet Jake's greatest gift was forcing me to make a choice: perform the heinous act or quit. In so choosing, I learned to take risks without fearing failure, and now it is confidence, not fear, that propels me forward - whether playing basketball, or playing Heathcliffe in Disco Inferno.

    What began as an act of rebellion became the most fulfilling adventure of my life.



    Selected Projects

    File Compression

    This project is a recreation of a form of lossless file compression developed by David Huffman. Instead of using 7 bits to encode each character, as ASCII does, it uses a variable-length encoding of characters. Frequently occurring characters get shorter code words than infrequently occurring ones.

    Sketch Server

    This is a collaborative graphical editor built using the swing library. Multiple clients connect to a server, and whatever editing any of them does, the others see. It handles multiple objects at a time, and can draw rectangles, line segments, and "freehand" shapes in addition to ellipses.

    Pixel Manipulation

    In this program, the user clicks on a pixel in the webcam to select a color. We then employ a "region growing" algorithm to expand outward from the selected pixel, detecting and recoloring all other pixels of a similar color. In painting mode, the user can use the detected region as a paintbrush on the webcam.

    Collision Detection

    This project recreates an efficient approach to detecting collisions in a two-dimensional space. The inefficient approach is to check each object to see if it's colliding with another object. Instead, just as a binary search tree enables faster look-up than linear search in one dimension, a point quadtree enables faster look-up in two dimensions.

    Slack.com Landing Page

    This recreation (with a few tweaks) of slack.com's landing page was an experiment through which I learned html5 and CSS3. It is iOS-friendly and includes all the responsive features of the original website.
